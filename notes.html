On restraint and light

March 12, 2026

Quiet images ask for room. In a season of noise, we keep returning to frames that withhold more than they show—light held at the edge, gesture half-finished.

This note is about that habit: restraint as a way of seeing, not a pose.

In the studio we test how little a picture can carry and still feel full. That often means slower shoots, fewer setups, and a willingness to stop before the frame is “busy enough” for the feed.

When we look back at months of work, the images that stay with us are rarely the loudest. They are the ones that still ask a question after you have looked away.

Between two cities

February 4, 2026

The studio works across Paris and New York—two rhythms, two kinds of light, one thread of attention. We do not pretend there is a single center.

What holds is the practice: show up, edit hard, leave the rest open.

Flights and time zones are a logistics problem; the harder part is keeping one eye on the long project while the other is on tomorrow’s brief. We borrow from each city without performing either as a cliché.

The work changes when the light does. Paris asks for a different patience than New York’s blunt winter sun. We try to let the place leave a trace in the frame, not in the caption.

What we keep in the edit

January 18, 2026

Sequencing is where the work becomes honest. Some frames stay in the folder for months; others never leave the first pass.

Pacing, cuts, and what we refuse to explain out loud—that is the edit, and it is where the voice settles.

We often print a rough sequence and live with it for a week. What reads as “fine” on screen can feel thin on paper; the edit is partly about finding a rhythm that survives a body moving through the room.

The last pass is not polish—it is a refusal. We take out the image that flatters the idea and keep the one that complicates it. That is how a project stops being a mood board and becomes a body of work.
